## Policy Overview

The first half of 2026 has seen major trading economies introduce significant trade policy changes with far-reaching implications for export supply chains and compliance management.

## Major Policy Changes

### US Tariff Adjustments

The USTR conducted Section 301 tariff reviews for multiple product categories including electronics, machinery, and chemicals. While some tariff rates have been reduced, compliance requirements have tightened significantly, particularly for rules of origin documentation.

### EU Customs Upgrade

The European Commission introduced a comprehensive customs reform package:

- **Data-First Approach**: Full digital declaration data required 48 hours before cargo arrival
- **AI Risk Assessment**: New AI-driven risk system for enhanced high-risk cargo screening
- **Simplified Clearance**: Faster clearance channels for AEO-certified low-risk goods

### China Export Controls

China's Ministry of Commerce updated Export Control Law implementing regulations, expanding the controlled items list to include AI chips, quantum computing equipment, and industrial software. Enterprises must reassess export product classifications.

### Cross-border Data Compliance

Multiple countries strengthened cross-border data regulations:

- **EU**: Data Governance Act imposes new requirements on logistics data transfers
- **China**: Clarified data export security assessment standards
- **Southeast Asia**: Indonesia and Vietnam enacted data localization laws

## Recommendations

1. Establish policy monitoring mechanisms tracking changes in key markets
2. Update compliance systems with latest tariff codes, control lists, and declaration requirements
3. Strengthen supply chain flexibility with contractual contingency clauses
4. Invest in digital customs capabilities including EDI and smart declaration platforms
5. Conduct regular compliance training for customs, procurement, and logistics teams
